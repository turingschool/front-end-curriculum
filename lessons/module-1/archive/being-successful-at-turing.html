Hello.

Welcome to the Front-End Engineering Program.

We’re going to talk a little bit about how to be successful at Turing.

Traditional School

  • Memorize some facts
  • Take some tests
  • Quietly listen to someone ~~drone on~~ teach
  • Figure out the one right answer or way to do something

Educational Approach

  • You learn by doing
  • Gradual release of responsibility
  • Spiraling of expertise
  • Community-driven education

Modules

  • 6 weeks.
  • Each is pass/fail.
  • Build up a portfolio.

Modules

  • Module 1 and Module 2 are highly structured.
  • Module 3: more independence.
  • Module 4: very independent.

It’s that whole “gradual release of responsibility.”

Weeks

  • Monday & Tuesday are more instructional.
  • Wednesday & Thursday have more work and evaluation/feedback time.
  • Friday is community day.

Classes

  • Typical class is 90-minutes with one instructor focused on one topic.
  • Classes are supposed to be “just in time” to apply the skill to your project.
  • Classes may be lecture, code-along, tutorial, small group work, etc.

Projects

  • Projects are where you cement your learning
  • Typically 1-2 weeks long
  • Evaluated by an instructor
  • Building blocks of your portfolio

This is going to be a little different.

Yes, some basic skills (e.g. syntax) should be memorized.

But, you can always reference the documentation.

  • There are multiple ways to complete a task.
  • There is rarely one right answer.
  • You learn by doing the thing.

You will be challenged.

You’re going to get frustrated at times.

There will be some times of uncertaintly.

And it’s all on purpose.

Sorry—but, we’re not sorry.

The goal here is to not waste your time.

Some people ask how is it possible to learn to do all of this stuff in like 7 months? Because it’s super concentrated.

Do we believe everyone can do it? No.

We have an admissions process. We’ve been refining it for two years. You got in.

Everyone struggled.

Everyone still struggles.

Questions

Ask lots of them.

Don’t worry about asking “stupid questions.” (We already know you’re not stupid.)

Get over your ego. (You’re not good enough just yet to not ask questions.)

Be willing to publicly fail.

Be willing to be wrong.

Allow others to publicly fail.

Allow others to be wrong.

Be ready to not get an answer.

Why not just go to the instructor and get the answer instead of struggling for a bit?

(Something about giving people fish and teaching them to fish, I suppose.)

As instructors, we don’t necessarily have the answers.

Each situation is unique.

There might be multiple answers to one question.

Hey, there might even been multiple questions to one answer.

(I don’t really know what that means, but it felt smart when I wrote it.)

How to Ask for Help

  1. Explain the problem you encountered.
  2. Identify the error messages you got.
  3. Explain what steps you’ve already taken and what the outcome was.
